Private Equity

Learn what private equity is, how funds invest in companies, what the work involves, and how students can prepare for internships and analyst roles.

View vacancies Explore finance paths

Introduction

What is private equity?

Private equity means investing money directly into companies that are not listed on a stock exchange. Investors buy a stake in a business, work to make it more valuable over a number of years, and then sell that stake, ideally at a profit. The money comes from a private equity fund, which pools capital from large investors such as pension funds, insurers and wealthy individuals.

What it isInvesting in private companies, or buying a listed company and taking it private, with the aim of growing its value and selling later.
How funds make moneyBy improving how a company performs and buying and selling at the right price. Firms often use debt to finance a purchase, which can raise the return on their own capital.
Why companies sellA founder retires, a family cashes out, a group sells a division, or a business needs capital and expertise to grow. Private equity offers money and hands-on support in exchange for ownership.
Why students like itIt combines finance, strategy and real ownership of business decisions. The work is analytical and varied, responsibility comes early, and the pay is among the highest in finance.

What they do

What private equity firms do

Deal sourcing Finding companies worth buying, through networks, advisors and research.
Company analysis Understanding how a business earns, where it can grow and what could go wrong.
Valuation Working out what a company is worth today and what it could be worth at exit.
Financial modelling Building models that test assumptions and estimate returns under different scenarios.
Due diligence Checking the numbers, contracts, market and risks in detail before a deal closes.
Debt financing Arranging the loans that often form part of how an acquisition is paid for.
Portfolio support Working with the companies already owned to improve results after the deal.
Exit planning Deciding how and when to sell a company to realise the investment.

Step by step

How a private equity deal works

1 Finding a targetThe firm identifies a company that fits its strategy and return goals.
2 Analysing the companyThe team studies the business model, market, financials and risks.
3 Valuing the businessAnalysts estimate a fair price using models and comparable deals.
4 Arranging financingThe purchase is funded with a mix of the fund capital and debt.
5 Buying the companyTerms are negotiated, contracts are signed and the deal closes.
6 Improving the companyThe firm supports management to grow revenue, cut costs and add value.
7 Selling the companyAfter a few years the stake is sold to another buyer or the public market.

Types

Types of private equity firms

Large-cap private equityGlobal firms that buy large, established companies in very big deals.
Mid-market private equityFirms focused on medium-sized businesses, a large part of the Dutch market.
Small-cap private equityInvestors in smaller companies, often with strong local knowledge.
Growth equityCapital for established, growing companies that want to expand faster.
Sector-focused fundsFirms that invest only in one area, such as technology, healthcare or industry.
Family-backed investment firmsInvestment vehicles that manage the wealth of a family or a small group.
Search fundsA single entrepreneur raises money to buy and then run one company.

Roles

Private equity roles

Intern

A student who supports the team with research, data and analysis.

Main tasks
Research, data gathering, market maps, model support.
Key skills
Excel, accounting basics, curiosity.
Typical level
Student, often in a later study year.

Analyst

An early-career hire who does much of the research and modelling.

Main tasks
Company research, financial models, valuation, diligence support.
Key skills
Modelling, valuation, attention to detail.
Typical level
Entry level, often from university or banking.

Associate

Runs the day-to-day of a deal and guides analysts.

Main tasks
Leads analysis, manages diligence, writes investment memos.
Key skills
Strong modelling, judgement, project management.
Typical level
A few years of experience, often after banking or consulting.

Investment Manager

Takes ownership of parts of a deal and of portfolio companies.

Main tasks
Deal execution, portfolio work, some sourcing.
Key skills
Deal experience, commercial judgement.
Typical level
Mid level.

Principal

Leads deals and shapes investment decisions.

Main tasks
Sourcing, negotiation, leading deal teams.
Key skills
Origination, negotiation, leadership.
Typical level
Senior.

Partner

Sets strategy, raises funds and holds final responsibility.

Main tasks
Fundraising, final decisions, representing the firm.
Key skills
Track record, relationships, leadership.
Typical level
Most senior.

Compare

Private equity versus other finance paths

Private equity vs investment banking

Banking advises on deals and raises capital for clients. Private equity invests its own fund and owns the companies, so the focus shifts from execution to long-term value.

Private equity vs venture capital

Venture capital backs young, fast-growing companies with high risk. Private equity usually buys mature, profitable businesses and often uses debt.

Private equity vs corporate finance

Corporate finance sits inside a company and manages its funding and strategy. Private equity is on the investor side, buying and improving companies.

Private equity vs asset management

Asset management invests mainly in listed shares and bonds for many clients. Private equity invests in private companies over longer horizons.

Private equity vs transaction services

Transaction services checks the numbers behind a deal for a buyer. Private equity is the buyer, deciding whether and how to invest.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

What is private equity?

Private equity is investing directly in companies that are not listed on a stock exchange. Investors buy a stake, work to grow the company value over several years and then sell it.

How do private equity firms make money?

They earn from improving and reselling companies at a higher value, often using debt to raise returns. The firm also charges a management fee and keeps a share of the profit.

Is private equity harder to enter than investment banking?

Usually yes. Firms are smaller and hire fewer people, and many roles expect prior experience, often from investment banking or consulting.

Can students apply directly to private equity?

Some firms offer internships and working student roles, especially smaller and mid-market funds. Many analyst roles are filled by people who first worked in banking or consulting.

Do you need investment banking first?

Not always, but it is the most common route. Banking teaches modelling, valuation and deal work. Consulting, transaction services and corporate finance are other routes.

What is an LBO?

A leveraged buyout is buying a company using a large amount of borrowed money. The debt is repaid from the company cash flow, which can increase the return on the investor own capital.

What skills should I learn first?

Start with accounting and valuation, then financial modelling in Excel. Add clear writing and commercial thinking. These are the foundations for almost every task.

Which internships help most?

Internships in investment banking, transaction services, corporate finance, valuation or a smaller private equity fund are the most useful.
